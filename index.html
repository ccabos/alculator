How the BAC estimate works

Alculator models your blood-alcohol concentration (BAC) minute by minute using established pharmacokinetic equations. This page explains every step.

1. Ethanol dose

The mass of pure ethanol in a drink is calculated from its volume and alcohol-by-volume (ABV) rating, using the physical density of ethanol (0.789 g/mL):

Ethanol (g) = Volume (mL) × ABV/100 × 0.789

For example, a 330 mL beer at 5 % ABV contains 330 × 0.05 × 0.789 ≈ 13.0 g of ethanol.

2. Volume of distribution — the Widmark r factor

Ethanol distributes throughout the body water, not just the bloodstream. The Widmark r factor (also called the apparent volume of distribution) describes what fraction of body mass effectively dilutes the alcohol. A larger body-water compartment means the same dose produces a lower BAC.

When height is provided, Alculator uses the Seidl et al. (2000) regression, which is more accurate than the population-mean fallback:

Male:   r = 0.31608 − 0.004821 × W + 0.4632 × H
Female: r = 0.31223 − 0.006446 × W + 0.4466 × H

where W is weight in kg and H is height in metres. When height is not known, Widmark's (1932) population means are used instead (male: 0.68, female: 0.55).

The Widmark formula then converts ethanol mass to BAC:

BAC (%) = Ethanol (g) / (Weight (kg) × 1000 × r) × 100

3. Absorption

Alcohol is not absorbed instantly. It passes through the stomach into the small intestine, where most absorption occurs. The speed depends on gastric emptying, carbonation, and food in the stomach.

3a. Linear ramp model

For a drink consumed quickly (instantaneous model), absorption follows a linear ramp from 0 % to 100 % over a window Tabsorb:

f(elapsed) = clamp(elapsed / Tabsorb, 0, 1)

Base absorption windows (fasted, no food): 45 min for still drinks; 20 min for carbonated drinks. Carbonation accelerates gastric emptying (Ridout et al., 2003), so beer and champagne reach the bloodstream faster.

3b. Extended-duration model

When a drink is sipped over a period D minutes, each sip starts its own Tabsorb ramp at the moment it is consumed. The total absorbed fraction is the convolution of a uniform intake rate with the linear ramp, computed analytically:

f(elapsed) = [ H(elapsed) − H(elapsed − D) ] / D

where H(x) = 0           if x ≤ 0
               x² / (2T)  if 0 < x ≤ T
               x − T/2   if x > T

This closed-form solution has no numerical error, correctly starts absorbing from the first sip, and finishes at elapsed = D + Tabsorb (last sip fully absorbed). It reduces continuously to the instantaneous formula as D → 0.

4. Food effects

Food in the stomach affects alcohol in two ways:

  • Slower gastric emptying — food delays alcohol reaching the small intestine, spreading absorption over a longer window. This means more alcohol is eliminated concurrently with absorption, reducing the peak BAC.
  • Increased first-pass metabolism (FPM) — gastric alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) metabolises some ethanol in the stomach before it ever reaches the bloodstream. Food stimulates gastric ADH activity, increasing this pre-systemic breakdown (Frezza et al., 1990).

Both effects are captured together: a meal-size-dependent Tabsorb (Levitt & Levitt, 2020) and an ethanol_factor multiplier (< 1.0) that reduces the effective dose:

Snack:     T = 60 min, factor = 0.97, covers drinks for 60 min
Light meal: T = 75 min, factor = 0.95, covers drinks for 90 min
Full meal:  T = 90 min, factor = 0.92, covers drinks for 150 min
Heavy meal: T = 120 min, factor = 0.90, covers drinks for 180 min

Food coverage rules

A food event covers a drink (and its modifiers apply) if:

  • Case A — food was in the stomach at or before the drink was started, and the drink was consumed within the food event's post-window: tfood ≤ tdrink ≤ tfood + post_window.
  • Case B — the drink was started before food arrived, but absorption was still ongoing when food entered (i.e. the drink had not yet been fully absorbed): tdrink < tfood and tdrink + Tbase ≥ tfood.

When multiple food events cover the same drink, the most protective one wins (lowest ethanol_factor; tie-break: highest Tabsorb). The per-drink "eating alongside" checkbox acts as a coarse fallback when no timestamped food event covers the drink.

5. Elimination — Michaelis-Menten kinetics

Alcohol is primarily metabolised in the liver by the enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH). At the blood concentrations typical of social drinking, ADH is nearly saturated, and elimination proceeds at a roughly constant rate. This is why BAC drops linearly over most of a drinking session. However, at very low BAC (< ~10 mg/dL), ADH becomes unsaturated and elimination slows, causing the curve to tail off smoothly rather than abruptly hit zero.

The classic Widmark β model assumes purely constant (zero-order) elimination, which overestimates the rate at low BAC and creates an unrealistic hard floor. Alculator instead uses the Michaelis-Menten equation (Holford, 1987; Norberg et al., 2003), which captures both regimes:

βeff(c) = βmax × c / (c + Km)
  • βmax = 0.015 % BAC/hour — maximum elimination rate (population mean; range 0.010–0.020)
  • Km = 0.015 % BAC — ADH half-saturation constant (~10–15 mg/dL)

At high BAC (c ≫ Km), βeff → βmax, giving zero-order kinetics identical to Widmark. At low BAC (c ≪ Km), βeff → βmax × c / Km, giving first-order kinetics: the rate drops proportionally with BAC, and the curve smoothly approaches zero.

6. The simulation loop

The model runs minute by minute from the first drink to two hours beyond the present (extended further if needed to find sober time). At each minute:

BAC[t] = max(0, BAC[t−1] + ΔBACabs(t) − ΔBACelim(t))

where:

  • ΔBACabs(t) = sum over all drinks of ethanol_g × ethanol_factor × Δf(t) / (weight_kg × 1000 × r) × 100, with Δf(t) = f(t − tdrink) − f(t − 1 − tdrink) being the fraction absorbed in this one-minute step.
  • ΔBACelim(t) = βeff(BAC[t−1]) / 60, the Michaelis-Menten decrement for one minute.

7. Uncertainty

Individual biological variability in pharmacokinetics is substantial. Factors such as genetic variation in ADH isoforms, body composition measurement error, stomach contents, hydration, and metabolic rate all affect real BAC. Based on Gullberg (2015), the population coefficient of variation for BAC estimates is approximately ±21 %.

The uncertainty band shown on the chart and in the header is:

Lower = BAC × (1 − 0.21)
Upper = BAC × (1 + 0.21)

The caution warning ⚠ appears when the upper bound exceeds 0.8 ‰, meaning there is a plausible scenario where your true BAC is at or above the common legal driving limit even if the central estimate is below it.

8. Sober time

The "Sober by" time is the first minute after which BAC remains below 0.001 % (effectively zero) for the rest of the simulated series. If BAC is still above this threshold at the two-hour forecast horizon, the simulation is extended by a further six hours to find the crossing point.
